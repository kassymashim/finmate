# Budgeting Strategies

## The 50/30/20 Rule
The 50/30/20 budget rule divides your after-tax income into three categories:
- 50% for Needs: Essential expenses like housing, utilities, groceries, transportation, insurance, and minimum debt payments.
- 30% for Wants: Non-essential spending like dining out, entertainment, shopping, hobbies, and subscriptions.
- 20% for Savings & Debt Repayment: Emergency fund contributions, retirement savings, extra debt payments, and investment contributions.

This rule works best for people who want a simple framework. If you live in a high-cost area, you may need to adjust to 60/20/20 temporarily.

## Zero-Based Budgeting
Every dollar of income is assigned a job before the month begins. Income minus all planned expenses equals zero. This method forces intentional decisions about every purchase and works well for people who want maximum control.

Steps:
1. List all income sources for the month
2. List all fixed expenses (rent, utilities, subscriptions)
3. Estimate variable expenses (groceries, gas, entertainment)
4. Assign remaining money to savings goals
5. Track actual spending vs. plan throughout the month

## Envelope Method (Digital Version)
Allocate specific amounts to spending categories. When an envelope is empty, no more spending in that category until next month. Digital versions use separate accounts or app-based tracking.

## Pay Yourself First
Before paying any bills or discretionary spending, automatically transfer a set percentage (typically 10-20%) to savings. This ensures savings happen consistently regardless of spending habits.

## The 80/20 Budget
Save 20% of income first, then spend the remaining 80% however you want without tracking individual categories. Best for people who find detailed budgeting too restrictive.

## Anti-Budget Method
Only track one number: the amount you want to save each month. Automate that transfer, then spend the rest guilt-free. Works for high earners who don't overspend but want simplicity.
